Prologue

I made this so I wouldn't forget.

The textbook tells you what managerial accounting is. It doesn't tell you which parts come back later, in a job interview or a meeting where someone wants to know if a number on a slide is real. One thing that surprised me by the end: most of these chapters are the same income statement, rearranged. CVP, variable costing, differential analysis, flexible budgets all start from the same place. Once I noticed that, the class stopped feeling like a stack of disconnected topics.

The chapters labeled High Utility are the ones I'd revisit first if I had to pick.

Chapter
01

Cost concepts

The vocabulary the rest of the class is built on. Classify a cost three different ways and most of what comes after gets easier.

In Practice
"Is this fixed or variable?" comes up in pricing, hiring, layoffs, scaling decisions. It's the most-used question from this entire class.
Watch For
The word factory. It decides whether a cost is product or period.

Same cost, three different labels. None of them wrong. Each useful for a different question.

Three classifications

By function in production

Direct Materials (DM)Raw materials traceable to the product. Lumber in a table, steel in a car.
Direct Labor (DL)Wages of workers physically building the product.
Manufacturing Overhead (MOH)Every other factory cost. Rent, utilities, supervisor salary, glue, the janitor.

By timing on the income statement

Product CostsDM + DL + MOH. Sit in inventory until the product sells, then become COGS.
Period CostsSelling, admin, executive salaries, advertising. Hit the income statement immediately.

By behavior with volume

TotalPer Unit
VariableChanges with volumeStays the same
FixedStays the sameDrops as volume rises
MixedHas both. Usually written as y = mx + b
The rule that fixes most classification errors

If a cost happens at the factory and isn't DM or DL, it's MOH. Sales offices, corporate HQ, delivery trucks: period costs.

Two combined terms

Prime Cost
DM + DL

The most directly traceable production cost. The "prime ingredients and people" view.

Conversion Cost
DL + MOH

What it takes to convert raw materials into finished goods. Direct labor sits in both. It's the only line item that does.

Common trap

Variable cost per unit is constant. Variable cost in total grows with volume. The total grows because every new unit eats the same per-unit amount.

Chapter
05

Cost-volume-profit

Probably the most reused tool from this class. Break-even, target profit, pricing math. Anything that asks "what happens to profit when volume changes?" lives here.

In Practice
Startups use this to set runway. Restaurants use it to price menus. SaaS companies use it for free-trial conversion math.
Quick Check
Want answer in units? Use CM per unit. Want answer in dollars? Use CM ratio. Mismatch produces nonsense.

CVP is one rearrangement of the income statement. Once you put it in contribution margin form, every CVP question is a small algebra problem.

The contribution margin format

Take a regular income statement and reorganize so all variable costs come out first. What's left is the contribution margin: dollars that go toward covering fixed costs and generating profit.

Income statement, rearranged
Sales − Variable Costs = Contribution Margin Contribution Margin − Fixed Costs = Operating Income

The four formulas

Contribution margin
CM per unit = SP − VC per unit CM Ratio = CM ÷ Sales
Break-even
Units = Fixed Costs ÷ CM per unit Dollars = Fixed Costs ÷ CM Ratio
Target profit (add target to numerator)
Units = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) ÷ CM per unit Dollars = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) ÷ CM Ratio
Margin of safety
MOS = Actual Sales − Break-even Sales
The mental model

Each unit sold contributes CM toward covering fixed costs. Once fixed costs are covered, every additional CM dollar is profit.

Regression for cost behavior

When utility or maintenance costs don't fit cleanly as fixed or variable, regression splits them. Output looks like y = 1.92x + 675. Intercept is fixed cost. Slope is variable cost per unit of activity.

R² ValueRead as
0.85 to 1.00Strong. Model is a reliable predictor.
0.50 to 0.85Moderate. Some predictive power.
Below 0.50Weak. Don't trust the model alone.

Exam-ready phrasing: "R² of 0.857 means 85.7% of the variation in cost is explained by the activity driver."

"Margin of safety asks how far above break-even we are. Target profit asks what we need to hit a goal. Different questions, different inputs."
A common confusion, neutralized
Chapter
13

Differential analysis

Five different decisions, one question that solves all of them.

In Practice
"Should we keep this product?" "Outsource this team?" "Take this deal?" Differential analyses, even when no one calls them that.
Always Ignore
Sunk costs. Unavoidable fixed costs. Allocated common overhead.

Every differential analysis comes down to one question: what changes between the two options? Whatever changes is relevant. Whatever stays the same isn't.

What's never relevant

Sunk costsAlready spent. The decision can't change them. Recent doesn't mean relevant.
Unavoidable fixed costsWill happen no matter which option you pick.
Allocated common overheadJust gets reassigned to other segments if you drop one.

The five decisions

Decision 01
Drop a segment

Compare CM lost against avoidable fixed costs saved. Drop only if savings exceed CM lost.

Decision 02
Make or buy

Cost to make (DM + DL + VMOH + avoidable FMOH + opportunity cost) versus the supplier's price.

Decision 03
Special order

Incremental revenue against incremental cost. Fixed costs usually don't matter if there's spare capacity.

Decision 04
Constrained resource

Rank by CM per unit of the constraint, not per unit of product. Highest wins.

Decision 05 — Sell or process further
Process further if: Incremental revenue > Incremental cost (Joint costs up to split-off are sunk. Ignore.)
The biggest trap in this chapter

A segment that shows a net loss is not automatically a candidate to drop. If its CM exceeds its avoidable fixed costs, dropping it makes the company worse off, because the unavoidable allocated costs just move to other segments.

The framework, condensed

Future and differs between options = relevant. Past or unchanged = irrelevant. That sentence solves the chapter.

Chapter
14

Capital budgeting

How to evaluate big investments. Shows up in corporate finance, real estate, MBA programs, and the question of whether to buy a house.

In Practice
NPV and IRR are two of the most universal tools in business. They show up in finance interviews, MBA programs, real estate, startup valuations.
Critical Habit
If a problem says "operating income," add back depreciation before doing anything else. All three tools use cash flow, not accounting income.

Three tools that ask slightly different questions. They almost always agree on accept or reject.

The depreciation add-back

Capital budgeting cares about cash, not accounting income. Depreciation reduces operating income but isn't a cash outflow. The cash already left when you bought the equipment. So if a problem hands you operating income, add depreciation back to get net cash flow.

Convert accounting income to cash flow
Net Cash Flow = Operating Income + Depreciation

Tool one — Payback period

How long until the investment pays itself back. Easy to compute. Useful as a quick screen. Ignores cash flows after the payback point and ignores time value of money.

Even cash flows
Payback = Initial Investment ÷ Annual Cash Flow
Uneven cash flows
Payback = Full years before recovery + (Unrecovered ÷ Next year's flow)

Tool two — Net Present Value

Discounts every future cash flow back to today's dollars at the required rate of return. The most reliable single metric. Accept if NPV ≥ 0.

NPV with three cash flow types
NPV = PV of inflows − Initial investment Initial investment → factor 1.000 Annual cash flows → PV annuity factor Salvage / one-time amounts → PV of $1 factor

Tool three — Internal Rate of Return

The rate at which NPV equals zero. The implied return the project is generating. Accept if IRR ≥ required rate.

IRR shortcut for even cash flows
PV Factor = Initial Investment ÷ Annual Cash Flow Look up factor in PV annuity table at the project's life Read the column header. That's IRR.
IRR > required rate ⇔ NPV > 0.
They always agree on accept or reject for a standard project.
The Golden Identity
ToolStrengthWeakness
PaybackSimple. Screens for liquidity risk.Ignores time value. Ignores post-payback cash.
NPVMost reliable. Accounts for time value.Less intuitive than a percentage.
IRREasy to communicate as a rate.Can mislead with non-standard cash flow patterns.
Chapter

ROI & responsibility

How decentralized organizations evaluate divisions and managers. The DuPont identity (Margin × Turnover) is one of the most-cited frameworks in finance.

In Practice
Public companies report ROI by segment. PE firms evaluate portfolio companies on it. Bonus structures often tie to it.

ROI tells you how hard a division's assets are working. Two ways to calculate it. One shows the answer. The other shows the levers.

Two equivalent paths to ROI
ROI = NOI ÷ Avg Operating Assets ROI = Margin × Turnover where: Margin = NOI ÷ Sales Turnover = Sales ÷ Avg Operating Assets

Three responsibility centers

CenterManager controlsExample
CostCosts onlyManufacturing plant
ProfitCosts + revenue (no investments)Amusement park
InvestmentCosts + revenue + capitalDivision VP
The ROI gaming problem

A division with high ROI may reject a profitable investment because it would lower the division's average ROI, even if the project beats the company's required return. ROI alone can push managers to turn down good projects to protect their scorecard.

The DuPont insight

You can boost ROI by improving margin (charge more or cost less) or by improving turnover (work the same assets harder). Most companies have a clearer path through one than the other.

Chapter
09

Flexible budgets & variances

A way to evaluate performance fairly. Separates "we sold a different volume than planned" from "we charged or spent the wrong amount per unit."

In Practice
Quarterly business reviews. The "why did we miss our number?" conversation is a flexible budget variance, formalized.

Comparing actuals to a planning budget isn't fair. Part of the difference is just because volume changed. The flexible budget fixes that by recalculating the budget at the volume that actually happened.

The three budgets

BudgetVolumePricesQuestion it answers
PlanningPlannedPlannedWhat we expected
FlexibleActualPlannedWhat costs should have been at this volume
ActualActualActualWhat really happened
Two variances, two stories
Activity Variance = Planning Budget − Flexible Budget (impact of volume changing) Spending/Revenue Variance = Flexible Budget − Actual (impact of per-unit prices changing)
Memory anchor

Activity is what changed because activity (volume) was different.
Spending is what changed because spending per unit was different.

Fixed costs always have an activity variance of zero. They don't flex with volume. All variance on fixed costs flows through the spending variance.

Chapter

Standard costs & variances

Four formulas, one skeleton. Each variance asks: what's different from standard, and why?

In Practice
Manufacturing controllers live here. Less universal than CVP, but if you ever work in operations or COGS analysis, this is daily vocabulary.

Materials and labor each have two possible reasons for missing standard: a different price or rate, or a different quantity. Four variances total. Two questions, two inputs.

The four formulas

VarianceFormulaOwner
Materials Price(AP − SP) × AQ purchasedPurchasing
Materials Quantity(AQ used − SQ allowed) × SPProduction
Labor Rate(AR − SR) × AHHR / Hiring
Labor Efficiency(AH − SH allowed) × SRProduction supervisor
The pattern

Price and rate variances multiply by actual quantity, because the price mistake is made on every real unit. Quantity and efficiency variances multiply by standard price, to keep price effects out of a quantity variance.

Standard quantity allowed
SQ allowed = Standard per unit × Actual units produced

The "allowed" piece flexes the standard to actual output. You don't punish production for using less material just because they made fewer units than forecasted.

The connected story

An unfavorable price variance often causes a favorable quantity variance. Better materials waste less. Don't evaluate either variance in isolation. They often share a single decision.

Chapter

Master budgeting

A network of interconnected budgets. Each one's output feeds the next. Sales is always the start.

In Practice
Annual planning at every company. The mechanics get automated in spreadsheets, but the logic and the cash-lag tables remain manual.

Sales budget. Production budget. Materials budget. Cash budget. Mess up an early one and everything downstream breaks.

The one core formula (appears three times)
What you NEED + What you WANT at end − What you HAVE at start = What you must GET

The chain

BudgetApplication
ProductionSales + Desired EI − Beginning Inv = Units to produce
DM PurchasesProduction needs + Desired EI material − Beginning material = Material to buy
The chain rule

Ending inventory of one month is the beginning inventory of the next. The numbers don't change. Only the label does.

Cash collections lag

If customers pay 65% in the month of sale and 35% the next month, build a grid. Each row is a source month. Each column is a collection month. Don't try to do it in your head.

The setup that prevents most errors

Always lay out cash flow as a grid, never as a single calculation. Source month down the side. Collection month across the top. Same pattern works for cash disbursements.

Chapter

Variable vs absorption

Two methods that treat fixed manufacturing overhead differently. The difference shows up in operating income whenever production doesn't equal sales.

In Practice
Specialist territory. Important for accountants and CFOs. Useful to recognize, less useful to compute by hand.

The only difference between absorption and variable costing is how they treat fixed manufacturing overhead. Absorption puts it in inventory. Variable expenses it immediately.

AbsorptionVariable
Fixed MOHProduct cost (in inventory until sold)Period cost (expensed now)
DM, DL, VMOHProduct costProduct cost
Selling/AdminPeriod costPeriod cost
Required for GAAP?YesNo (internal use)

The reconciliation rule

ScenarioIncome comparison
Production > Sales (inventory builds)Absorption > Variable (FMOH stays in inventory)
Production = SalesAbsorption = Variable
Production < Sales (inventory shrinks)Absorption < Variable (last period's FMOH hits COGS)
Reconciliation formula
Absorption Income − Variable Income = (Δ inventory units) × FMOH per unit
The mental model

Absorption follows the units. If units stay in inventory, so does FMOH. If units leave (sold), so does FMOH.

Job-order costing

How manufacturing companies attach overhead to specific jobs and trace costs through three inventory accounts. Useful framework, narrower application.

In Practice
Manufacturing, custom construction, professional services billing. The flow concept (RM → WIP → FG → COGS) shows up anywhere goods move through stages.

Manufacturing overhead can't be traced to a specific job, so we estimate it and apply it using a predetermined rate. Then everything flows through three inventory accounts on its way to the income statement.

The predetermined overhead rate

POHR — set once at the start of the year
POHR = Estimated Total Overhead ÷ Estimated Allocation Base

Both numbers are estimates set at the start of the year. The allocation base is whatever the problem gives. Usually direct labor hours, direct labor cost, or machine hours.

Apply OH to a specific job
Applied OH = POHR × Actual allocation base for the job

Estimates make the rate. Actuals apply the rate.

Over- and under-applied overhead

Actual MOH − Total Applied MOH = Over/Under-applied amount
ResultMeaningYear-end fix
Actual > AppliedUnder-applied (didn't charge enough)Increase COGS
Actual < AppliedOver-applied (charged too much)Decrease COGS

The cost flow

Costs move through three inventory accounts in sequence. Each step uses the same logic: beginning + added − ending = flowed out.

Direct materials used
DM Used = Beg RM + RM Purchases − Ending RM − Indirect Materials
Cost of goods manufactured
COGM = Beg WIP + (DM + DL + MOH applied) − Ending WIP
Cost of goods sold
COGS = Beg FG + COGM − Ending FG
The pattern

Same logic, three different inventory accounts. Beginning + Added − Ending = Flowed Out. Once you see it once, you've seen it three times.
